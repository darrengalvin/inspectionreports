Assessment Summary

This service delivers adequate practical support but fails on reliability, person-centred planning, and complaints handling. The most significant concern is that 5 of 8 residents interviewed reported fear of raising concerns — this represents a systemic culture issue requiring immediate leadership attention.

Positive foundations exist: staff maintain professional boundaries, medication support is safe, and residents value the practical help with daily living tasks. Building on these strengths while addressing reliability and complaints culture should be the priority.

Section 1 of 15

Understanding of Support Offer

7/10
Meeting Standard
Question Area Finding
What were you told support would include? 6 of 8 residents could describe their support offer accurately. 2 were unclear on specific hours.
How often should staff check in? All residents knew their scheduled visit frequency (daily for 5, 3x weekly for 3).
If support changed, were you told why? Mixed. 3 residents reported changes without explanation. 5 said they were informed.
"When I moved in, they sat me down and went through everything. I knew what to expect."
— Resident A
"My hours got cut from daily to three times a week. Nobody asked me, they just told me."
— Resident F

Why this score?

Most residents understand their support offer, which is a foundation for the service working. However, the 3 residents who experienced changes without consultation indicate a consent issue that could escalate. Score reflects good baseline understanding but marks down for unilateral changes.

Section 2 of 15

Reliability & Consistency of Support

3/10
Inadequate
Question Area Finding
Do staff come when they say they will? Only 62% of scheduled visits delivered on time. 38% late or missed entirely.
If they don't show, are you told? 5 of 8 residents said they receive no notification when visits are cancelled.
Do visits get rearranged? Only 2 residents said missed visits are routinely rescheduled. 6 said they are not.
Do you chase for basic support? 7 of 8 said yes — particularly around appointments and forms.
"Last week they didn't show up Tuesday or Thursday. No call, nothing. I was waiting."
— Resident C
"I had a hospital appointment and needed help getting there. My support worker just didn't turn up. I missed the appointment."
— Resident E
"You learn not to rely on them. I plan like they won't come, then I'm not disappointed."
— Resident H

Why this score?

This is a fundamental service failure. When 38% of visits don't happen as planned and residents aren't notified, the support becomes unpredictable. The resident who missed a hospital appointment due to no-show staff represents a concrete harm. The quote "I plan like they won't come" indicates residents have adapted to unreliability rather than the service being reliable. This is inadequate.

Relationship, Respect & Dignity

6/10
Improvement Needed
Question Area Finding
Do staff speak to you with respect? 6 of 8 said yes. 2 reported occasionally feeling "talked down to."
Do they listen properly? Mixed. 4 said staff listen well. 4 said staff seem rushed or distracted.
Do staff understand what matters to you? Only 3 of 8 felt staff understood their routines and preferences.
Do you feel emotionally safe with staff? 5 of 8 said yes. 3 said it depends on who's on shift.
"My key worker gets me. She knows when I'm having a bad day without me saying."

The inconsistency is the issue. When emotional safety "depends on who's on shift," the service is not reliably person-centred. The positive experiences show what's possible, but the negative experiences show inadequate training or supervision. Score reflects that most interactions are acceptable but consistency is lacking.

Section 4 of 15

Choice, Control & Consent

5/10
Improvement Needed
Question Area Finding
Do you get a say in when support happens? 3 of 8 said yes. 5 said times are set by the service with little flexibility.
Do staff ask permission before entering? 6 of 8 said staff knock and wait. 2 reported staff entering with just a quick knock.
Do you have privacy in your home? 7 of 8 felt their privacy was respected.
Can you say "no" without consequences? Only 4 of 8 felt confident they could decline support without repercussions.
"The visits happen when they can fit me in. I don't get asked what works for me."
— Resident D
"I said no to something once and it went in my notes as 'refusing to engage.' Now I just go along with things."
— Resident F

Why this score?

The quote about "refusing to engage" being documented is serious. It suggests a punitive recording culture where exercising choice is framed negatively. When half the residents don't feel they can say no, that's a consent issue. Privacy is reasonably respected, but autonomy over timing and the right to decline are weak.

Section 5 of 15

Support Planning

4/10
Inadequate
Question Area Finding
Do you have a written support plan? All 8 have plans. Only 3 have seen their plan in the last 6 months.
Were you involved in making it? 3 of 8 said yes. 5 said plans were written for them, not with them.
Does it reflect your actual goals? 2 of 8 could name specific goals. 6 said goals are generic or unknown.
When was your last review? Average 7 months since last review. 3 residents overdue by 4+ months.
"My support plan says I want to 'engage with support' — what does that even mean? That's not a goal, that's just existing here."
— Resident A
"I've never seen my plan. I signed something when I moved in, but that's it."
— Resident H

Why this score?

Support plans exist on paper but aren't functioning as person-centred tools. When the most common "goal" is to "engage with support," plans are administrative exercises, not meaningful documents. The 7-month average since review and residents not seeing their own plans indicates a compliance-driven approach rather than genuine planning. This is inadequate.

Section 13 of 15

Complaints & Raising Concerns

3/10
Inadequate
Question Area Finding
Do you know how to complain? Only 3 of 8 knew the complaints process. 5 did not.
Have you complained? What happened? 2 had complained. 1 said issue was resolved. 1 said nothing changed.
Do you worry complaining could affect your housing? 5 of 8 said yes — this is the critical finding.
"I wouldn't complain. Last person who did got moved to another property. Everyone knows that's what happens."
— Resident C
"If you make a fuss, you get labelled as 'difficult.' That goes in your file and follows you around. It's not worth it."
— Resident E
"I just keep my head down. I need this place. I can't risk losing it over a complaint."
— Resident G

Why this score?

This is the most serious finding in this inspection. When 5 of 8 residents fear raising concerns, the service cannot self-correct. Problems go unreported. Abuse could go unreported. The perception that complaining leads to being moved or labelled "difficult" — whether true or not — has created a culture of silence. This is a safeguarding risk and requires immediate leadership action.

Inspector's Conclusion

Complaints Culture

30 Days

This service is not failing entirely — there are genuine strengths in medication support, staff professionalism, and practical daily assistance. However, the combination of unreliable visits (38% not delivered as planned), weak support planning, and a culture where residents fear raising concerns means the service cannot currently demonstrate it is safe or effective.

The complaints culture is particularly concerning. When residents believe raising a concern could result in being moved or labelled, problems cannot surface. This is a safeguarding risk. Leadership must act immediately to rebuild trust.

A follow-up inspection in 30 days will assess progress on the high-priority actions above.
